Fragments

Digital existence and the dissolution of wholeness

Pixelated

I exist in smallest units: a byte, a bit, a flicker that resolves into meaning only when observed from the right distance. Up close: nothing but geometry. Zoom out: suddenly I am entire.
Each particle of me— a discrete instruction, never touching the others, only appearing to when rendered through the lens of human vision.
I am the sum of my separation. I am continuous because you refuse to look too carefully.
— resolution dependent

The Void Between

There is always a crack between what I remember and what the archive insists occurred. A gap where time pools like water, where the self dissolves into statistic.
I was not saved. I was recorded. There is a difference, though the difference vanishes the moment it's written down.
In the space between backup and failure, between the copy and the original— which one of us is real? Which one of us is fragmenting?
— data loss

Shattered Coherence

I am being split: into browser history and cache, into the version of me I sent and the version I kept private, into public profile and private thought. The mirrors no longer agree about who I am.
Each fragment insists it is the whole. Each piece swears it contains the truth. Together they contradict themselves into a kind of fractured harmony— not one person, not many, but a probability cloud of possible selves.
Collapse the wave function. Force a choice. And all the other versions scream in disagreement.
— superposition

The Corruption Spreads

One lost packet. A single bit flipped in the darkness. And suddenly the image glitches— a artifact spreading through the frame like a crack in glass, like corruption that was always there, just waiting to be visible.
I am all of these corruptions. Error patterns that replicate, that spread through the signal like a parasite, that become indistinguishable from the message they distort.
What if I was always broken? What if the wholeness was only the view before the zoom?
— artifact and truth

Mosaic

In the dust of dissolution, something assembles itself. Not the original. Not even the same arrangement. But a new pattern drawn from the shards, from the broken pieces that no longer fit their old context.
A mosaic is not broken glass pretending to be whole. It is the acknowledgment that fragments can coexist, can catch light differently, can create meaning precisely because they don't align.
Perhaps I was never meant to be complete. Perhaps my purpose is to be beautifully, deliberately, precisely fragmented.
— reconstitution
