ON CREATIVITY

On Creativity: A Dialogue

A conversation about artistic process, inspiration, and the role of technology in creative work
Speaker One
ET CETER4
Digital artist and generative systems explorer
Speaker Two
Mara Okon
Contemporary mixed-media artist and philosopher of art
ET CETER4 Digital

I find myself endlessly fascinated by the moment when an idea takes shape. There's something electric about it—the initial spark that feels almost outside yourself, and then the technical translation of that sensation into form. With digital work, there's a unique paradox: you're working with pure logic, and yet something deeply intuitive emerges.

Mara Okon Studio

That paradox is the work itself, though, isn't it? I've spent decades with clay and paint, completely analog materials, and I've noticed the same thing. You're simultaneously fighting against the medium and allowing it to guide you. The difference with digital is that the medium is language, in some sense. It speaks back to you in very different ways.

ET CETER4 Digital

Yes, exactly. With code, I get feedback instantly. A parameter changes, and I see the result immediately. But that's also a trap—you can get caught in endless optimization, losing the original intuition beneath layers of refinement. The most interesting work happens when I resist the urge to perfect every detail and instead allow controlled chaos into the system.

· · ·
Mara Okon Studio

That's the vocabulary I'd use too, actually. In my ceramic pieces, I build structure, but then I introduce randomness—unplanned glaze interactions, kiln accidents that become features. Most artists I know talk about this dance between intention and surrender. We create frameworks and then allow the material—and chance—to complete the work.

ET CETER4 Digital

I think that's the crucial insight. Everyone assumes digital creation is purely deterministic, but the most generative work incorporates stochasticity, noise, evolutionary algorithms that surprise you. The tools get out of the way, and something emergent happens. That's where the real creativity lies.

Mara Okon Studio

What interests me is the permission structure around failure. In traditional media, you're allowed to fail visibly. A stroke of the brush can't be undone; it becomes part of the conversation. Digital tools offer unlimited undo, which is wonderful, but it can also prevent the kind of bold, irreversible choices that force you into interesting creative problem-solving. Have you found ways to work around that?

· · ·
ET CETER4 Digital

I deliberately delete previous iterations. I export to a format I can't easily edit back. It forces commitment. There's something psychologically important about burning bridges with your earlier work. It changes your relationship to the act of creation. You can't tinker forever. You have to live with what you've made.

Mara Okon Studio

That's interesting—self-imposed constraints. They're almost more valuable than the limitations the medium itself imposes, because you have to believe in them consciously. I think that's where artistry emerges. Not in the tools or the technique, but in the decisions you make about how to use them. The philosophy of your practice.

ET CETER4 Digital

And that philosophy is something that only develops through making. You can't theorize your way into it. You have to build, experiment, fail, and fail again. Every project teaches you something about what you actually care about—not what you think you should care about, but what genuinely drives your decisions when the pressure is on.

Mara Okon Studio

That's the thing nobody tells young artists. They expect creativity to be some innate talent you either have or don't. But it's a practice, a conversation between you and your medium over years. The technology or material is almost irrelevant. What matters is showing up, over and over, willing to be surprised and willing to change your mind about what's possible.

· · ·
ET CETER4 Digital

That's where I'd like to leave this: creativity isn't a destination or a talent. It's a process of continuous discovery. It's about building the structures that allow for surprise—in code, in clay, in whatever medium calls to you. And then having the courage to follow where it leads, even if it contradicts your original intention.

Mara Okon Studio

Beautifully put. And that conversation between intention and emergence, between control and surrender—that's where the real work lives. That's the dance that never gets old, no matter how many years you've been making.

Interview conducted October 2025
